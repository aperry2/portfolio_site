Technical Studies & Earlier Work

The works in this grouping are technical studies and standalone work not part of any cycle. They demonstrate my continuous engagement with media archaeology, beginning with film photography and traversing across computational image processing, electronics, 3D animation, and network infrastructure.

Tracing the Materiality of a Cellular Connection

This 2:02 video piece uses photogrammetry, cell network mapping research, and 3D animation in Autodesk Maya to explore the physical infrastructure that makes modern communication possible. The inspiration for this piece came while standing at a corner of a new home. From the 4th floor, I was able to physically stand in the path between two cell towers. I became curious about the journey of the electromagnetic waves carrying phone calls, texts, and many other forms of human communication as they passed through my body. Photogrammetric capture of cellular arrays was done with assistance from Emrys Brandt. This piece was screened at the 2023 SIDE/LOT Experimental Video Film Festival.

Portal

In this 4:31 short video piece, I employ Autodesk Maya renderings and FBX rigging from publicly accessible motion capture data to create a modern danse macabre with skeletons operating vintage computers and falling through the clouds. I composed the audio in Logic Pro in the Phrygian scale to evoke a sense of mysticism.

Pixel and Flower Studies

Over the course of roughly four years, I created pixel art inspired by research into the Bayer matrix dithering used for the Game Boy Camera from 1998. The images were either sourced from my own digital photography or publicly available botanical illustrations. In both cases, I used Processing to code custom software that operated on the pixels of the images directly. 12-by-12 inch inkjet prints were created from the processed images.

A Strange Game

This work was exhibited in a duo show at SITE Gallery titled Playing Chess in the Chinese Room, a play on philosopher John Searle’s “The Chinese Room” thought experiment (1980) that addressed the philosophy of mind applied to artificial intelligence. In this work, a chessboard is modified to include 64 sensors that are processed by an Arduino before being sent to a Raspberry Pi. The Pi continuously runs a Processing application that displays the live board state. During the exhibition opening, visitors were encouraged to play against a chess bot whose moves were enacted by myself, invoking the inverse image of the famed Mechanical Turk. 24-by-36 inch prints using the same visual language as the projection were hung in the gallery space. These represented each of the moves made in the 1997 Deep Blue v. Garry Kasparov match, when an artificial intelligence first beat the top human chess player.

Different Ways of Seeing

This body of work directly compares the visual features of the same buildings in Fort Collins, Colorado as rendered in Google Maps photogrammetric data and large format photography. The series of three 20-by-24 inch image pairs is my earliest attempt to synthesize photography with phenomenology.

After Prokudin-Gorskii

For this series I created custom software in Processing to glitch and double images from Sergei Prokudin-Gorskii’s early color photographic survey of the early 20th century Russian Empire. Re-enacting Prokudin-Gorskii’s red-green-blue color separation technique, I operated on each individual RGB color channel of the pixel array data. Images were sourced from the Library of Congress online database of his work. 12-by-12 inch inkjet prints were created from the processed images.

Wet Plate Collodion Work

In this series of work I created my own portable wet-plate collodion darkroom to produce wet-plate photographs of the landscape around the Colorado Rockies while researching the chemical antecedents of photography, like the modern myth of alchemist Albertus Magnus’ 13th century discovery of the light-sensitive qualities of silver nitrate. These unique images are 4-by-5 inch photographs on metal.

Methods of Manifestation

Representing the culmination of my BFA studies, I took large format landscape photographs of the Horsetooth Reservoir near Fort Collins, Colorado. Before printing in the darkroom, I modified the 4x5 negatives by applying heat to distort and destroy parts of the image. The series includes eight 8-by-10 inch prints and three 20-by-24 inch prints treated with selenium toner and espresso coffee baths.
